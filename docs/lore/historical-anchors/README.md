# Historical Anchor Candidates

This folder holds real historical anchors that can drive background sets. These
are not scripts for history illustrations. They are pressure sources: events
where suffering, death, upheaval, or loss changed ordinary life, and where the
art can show survival, repair, reuse, endurance, and environment after the fact.

The guiding rule:

> The tragedy happened, but the image does not show it directly. The image shows
> life continuing around its residue.

Use these anchors to create hidden scenarios for
[`../../background-set-briefs.md`](../../background-set-briefs.md). A set should
still withhold details. The historical event is for us, not for the viewer to be
able to identify from a caption.

## Candidate Anchors

### Black Death, Europe, 1347-1351

Fit: emptied villages, churchyards, labor disruption, roads and fields
continuing after enormous absence.

Survival / aftereffect: communities adapted; labor became more valuable in many
regions; ordinary religious and agricultural life continued around loss.

Residues: tended graves, empty chapel, overgrown lane, tools near fields, tiny
figure near a road, no visible bodies.

### Destruction Of Jerusalem And The Second Temple, 70 CE

Fit: sacred center destroyed, city broken, empire made visible as an enduring
structure, religious life forced to survive by transforming.

Survival / aftereffect: Jewish religious life continued without the Temple;
memory, law, teaching, exile, and rebuilt forms of practice carried life beyond
the catastrophe.

Residues: ruined temple stone, emptied courtyards, roads away from the city,
covered scrolls, teaching room without sanctuary, Roman administrative residue,
no battle scene.

Dedicated note: [`jerusalem-second-temple-70-ce.md`](jerusalem-second-temple-70-ce.md)

Set candidate: **After The Sanctuary**

### Great Famine, Europe, 1315-1317

Fit: rural bleakness, failed harvests, wet fields, hunger, and survival without
needing visible violence.

Survival / aftereffect: communal endurance, storage practices, monastic and
village food systems, fields returning after weather broke.

Residues: muddy roads, thin harvest, empty granary, covered baskets, rain-dark
fields, tiny figure in weather.

### Hundred Years' War, France and England, 1337-1453

Fit: repaired walls, fortified villages, old roads, abandoned watch points, and
farms growing around war residue.

Survival / aftereffect: local rebuilding, stronger institutions and identities,
ordinary life between campaigns.

Residues: patched gate, ruined wall with flowers, empty road, old tools,
defensive ditches, no battle.

### Hussite Wars, Bohemia, 1419-1434

Fit: religious conflict, wagons, fortified towns, chapel objects, rural roads,
belief as material pressure.

Survival / aftereffect: defensive ingenuity, lay religious identity, local
organization.

Residues: plain chapel objects, wagon road, empty meeting place, repaired
barricade, no fighters.

### Fall of Constantinople And Displacement, 1453

Fit: world-shifting loss without depicting the siege; people, books, craft, and
memory moving elsewhere.

Survival / aftereffect: preservation and transmission of knowledge, displaced
scholars and artisans, cultural continuation.

Residues: packed books, harbor road, closed gate, candlelit study, courtyard
with bundles, no siege scene.

### Dissolution Of The Monasteries, England And Wales, 1536-1540/1541

Fit: abandoned sacred spaces, stripped material culture, rural communities
losing monastic centers, stone and wood repurposed into ordinary life.

Survival / aftereffect: buildings and lands were reused; local life moved
through ruins; the material world persisted even after institutional life was
removed.

Residues: stripped chapel, empty cloister garden, reused stone, covered altar,
village road past broken abbey wall.

Dedicated note: [`dissolution-of-the-monasteries.md`](dissolution-of-the-monasteries.md)

### Thirty Years' War, Central Europe, 1618-1648

Fit: depopulated villages, mercenary routes, famine, repaired churches, fields
returning around violent residue.

Survival / aftereffect: rebuilding, local endurance, eventual peace structures.

Residues: empty village green, repaired church roof, old camp traces, quiet
field near ruins, no active soldiers.

### Great Fire Of London, 1666

Fit: tragedy and rebuilding purpose in an urban setting.

Survival / aftereffect: reconstruction, new planning, fire-prevention changes,
craft and labor returning to the city.

Residues: charred foundations, scaffold, stone blocks, tools, empty dawn street.

### Highland Clearances, Scotland, 18th-19th Century

Fit: rural beauty with human absence, empty homes, roads, sheep fields,
displacement.

Survival / aftereffect: diaspora, cultural persistence, memory carried
elsewhere.

Residues: abandoned croft, stone walls, cold road, distant faceless figure,
wind-heavy landscape.

### Irish Great Famine, 1845-1852

Fit: rural tragedy, empty cottages, roads to ports, fields, emigration, survival
under impossible conditions.

Survival / aftereffect: diaspora, memory, political awakening, endurance.

Residues: potato field, abandoned cottage, bundle by a wall, chapel, road to
port, no bodies.

### Year Without A Summer, 1816

Fit: weather-driven suffering, failed crops, strange skies, cold summer fields,
ordinary people surviving climate shock.

Survival / aftereffect: adaptation, migration, cultural and artistic echoes,
agricultural recovery.

Residues: frost on crops, dim sky, covered plants, empty road, tiny figure in
wrong-season weather.

Dedicated note: [`year-without-a-summer-1816.md`](year-without-a-summer-1816.md)

Set candidate: **The Summer That Failed**

### World War I Home Front / Rural Europe, 1914-1918

Fit: tragedy absent from the frame if focused on villages, fields, memorials,
railways, and ordinary survival.

Survival / aftereffect: rebuilding, remembrance, social change, fields still
tended.

Residues: empty railway platform, unreadable village memorial, repaired
farmhouse, fields worked by those left behind, no battlefield.

### Lijssenthoek / Remy Farm, World War I

Fit: a small rural farm-and-rail landscape became a major medical threshold
behind the Ypres front, useful because wounded people could arrive, be treated,
be sent onward, or be buried there.

Survival / aftereffect: ordinary care, records, transport, burial, nursing,
washing, and farm-space adaptation continued beside the unseen war.

Residues: farm roads, rail siding, white linen, canvas huts, water buckets,
horse or mule, covered stretchers, grave rows at a distance, no active combat.

Dedicated note: [`lijssenthoek-remy-farm-wwi.md`](lijssenthoek-remy-farm-wwi.md)

Set candidate: **The Farm Behind The Line**

## Selection Rule

Pick an anchor when it gives a set:

- a specific pressure, not a generic mood
- visible residues that do not explain too much
- a positive contrast of survival, repair, reuse, or ordinary life
- a way to avoid direct spectacle
- room for all six portrait archetypes to echo the scenario differently
