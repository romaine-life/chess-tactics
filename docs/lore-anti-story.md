# Lore And Anti-Story

This document records the thematic position behind the world art. It is not a
plot bible, faction guide, or campaign script. It exists because the project has
started to reveal a stranger identity than "fantasy chess": a medieval dream
made from chess pieces, meeples, roads, stone, wood, religion, weather, and
empty roles that still act with purpose.

The game should not become lore-heavy. The point is almost the opposite. Like
chess, it can remain one of the emptiest possible games: no hero, no villain, no
expansion-pack mythos, no named destiny. Yet chess cannot keep character out
entirely. The king, queen, bishop, rook, knight, and pawn are roles before they
are people, but they still carry culture with them. War figurines, religion,
stone, wood, roads, towers, graveyards, and village walls are so familiar that
they barely register as story. They count as "not really anything," and that is
where this world lives.

## Central Tension

The units are produced from chess pieces rendered in Blender, then gradually
stylized toward indie game art. This creates an important ambiguity. A pawn is
slightly more human than a normal pawn, but still obviously a pawn. A king or
queen may have slight garb or regalia, but remains a piece. These figures are
not simply people in costumes, and they are not simply inert objects. They exist
on the boundary between memory and object.

This ambiguity should remain unresolved. The figures may be memories. They may
be soulless reconstructions. They may be alive only because they are remembered.
It almost does not matter which is true. The world is interested in that edge:
remembered enough to move, not remembered enough to be whole.

There is an accidental contemporary echo here, almost a reference to AI. The
piece is a reconstruction that behaves as if it has life. It has form, role, and
objective, but no readable interior. That should not be turned into a literal
science-fiction explanation. It is a pressure underneath the image.

## Faceless Purpose

The pieces have no readable faces. That absence is not primarily sad, horrific,
peaceful, or accusatory. It is hollow and objective-driven. A pawn with no face
is not asking for pity; it is simply going somewhere.

Faces should never appear in readable form anywhere in the world art. Eyes,
mouths, expressions, saint faces, portrait faces, masks with expression, and
carved human faces are out. Noses may appear as the barest sculptural hint. A
figure may be turned away, hooded, too small to read, simplified into a pawn or
meeple, or reduced to a blank human-like silhouette. But the image should never
give the viewer a face to read.

The absence of faces makes the medieval material stranger. The period evoked by
chess, roughly 1000-1500 AD, carries hardship: no modern medicine, difficult
travel, weather, hunger, war, religion, hard labor, and short lives. The project
does not depict this as gothic danger. It treats it as part of ordinary life,
alongside beauty. A graveyard may sit beside a warm church. A ruined wall may
have flowers. A snowed-in road may be beautiful. Life has good and bad, and the
world is not afforded the speed of active drama.

## Dream Without Explanation

The whole endeavor is a dream, but not a dream that announces itself with magic.
The player does not need to be defined as a dreamer, commander, god-hand, child,
king, or observer. Defining the player too clearly would introduce too much
character. The experience should remain closer to chess: empty enough that the
player can project into it without being assigned a role.

The dream should pass at first glance. The scenery can be mostly realistic:
medieval towns, roads, chapels, towers, fields, rivers, snow, stone, and wood.
The viewer might look over an image and not notice anything out of the ordinary.
Only the details should become suspicious: a tiny pawn-like figure in a field
with no clear path, a road that seems ancient and practical but leads nowhere
obvious, a settlement with no visible inhabitants, a religious object that has
power because it is matter, not because it explains itself.

The image must pass before it haunts.

## Religion As Matter

Religion belongs in the world because medieval material culture is full of it,
and because it is as basic here as wood and stone. It should not appear as a
sermon, lore system, magical order, or explicit metaphysics. It appears as
chapels, grave markers, worn thresholds, bells, candle soot, carved doors,
monastery walls, reliquaries, cloisters, and the weight of old stone.

Religion has power because matter exists. A chapel wall is not a symbol that
needs explanation. It is a thing that remains.

## Roads And Ancient Movement

The world is not centered on chessboard imagery. Chess is too deliberately
rigid. The checkered board should stay in the gameplay interface, not leak into
the scenic world as a motif. The world art should instead imply the journey the
pieces experience: roads, paths, fields, walls, bridges, snow tracks, muddy
lanes, and old routes created because people repeatedly used them.

Roads should feel ancient and practical. They may be broken, hidden, swallowed
by grass or snow, or continued out of frame, but they should not feel like theme
park paths. The world itself creates the space through movement, not through a
visible chess grid.

## Life As Motif

The environments should be mostly empty. However, small life-forms should recur
as a motif. These are not ordinary background villagers with personal identity.
They should look like pawns, meeples, or human silhouettes compressed toward
meeple proportions: a human simplification still trying to be human.

They may appear isolated in nature, beside a road, near a wall, in a field, in
weather, or in a place with no clear route to or from it. They should not clarify
story. They should deepen the emptiness.

## War As Residue

War is allowed, but not as active spectacle. Active war is fast and energetic;
this world is not afforded that speed. Show the residue: walls, old banners,
empty camps, repaired damage, fortifications, rusted tools, abandoned weapons,
watchtowers, and fields that remember armies. Avoid battle tableaux, charging
forces, cinematic violence, gore, or grimdark emphasis.

## Backgrounds And Portraits

The project benefits from more artwork, and that is an intentional goal. The
meaning should not be squeezed into one all-purpose background. Instead it can
be distributed across a system:

- World and battle backgrounds carry broad atmosphere: empty medieval memory,
  weather, place, ordinary beauty, and suspicious details.
- Unit portrait backdrops carry archetype: the role's environment, material, and
  cultural residue without giving that piece a biography.
- Units carry the object/person boundary: faceless pieces that behave as living
  roles.
- UI carries chess-like restraint and tactical clarity.

Portrait backgrounds should use environmental archetypes rather than personal
scenes. The pawn can belong to a field edge or muddy road without becoming a
named peasant. The bishop can belong to chapel stone and grave flowers without
becoming a named priest. The rook can belong to walls and mass without becoming
a fortress commander.

These portrait backgrounds do not need to be continuous with the world
background that accompanies them. In many cases, it is better if they are not.
They should feel like fragments from the same dream-language rather than shots
from one mapped location. This gives the project a wider angle: it seems as if a
larger universe is being implied, but that promise is never fulfilled into plot,
geography, or biography. The images may suggest that some story is happening
somewhere, while the game refuses to deliver that story.

## Exclusions

The following break the dream:

- readable faces
- obvious magic effects, runes, portals, crystals, or spell language
- overt chessboard motifs in scenic backgrounds
- cute fairy-tale whimsy
- grimdark gore
- heroic fantasy poster energy
- modern objects
- named-character storytelling
- Disney-clean medieval polish
- images that explain the world too much

The palette should not obey a special lore rule. Each image should use the
palette that makes it good. Consistency comes from restraint, subject matter,
facelessness, material, absence, and atmosphere, not from forcing every scene
into one color grade.

## Working Thesis

This is a medieval dream-board where people have been simplified into roles.
Chess pieces and meeples are not costumes; they are how the dream remembers
human beings. The world is sometimes bright and pastoral, sometimes wintry,
ruined, religious, or empty, but always slightly hollow. Faces are absent, scale
is passable but not fully trustworthy, roads are old, matter has power, and
beauty does not cancel the unease of forgotten lives.
